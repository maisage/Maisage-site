Start with the basics

Understand the basics before you’re rushed into decisions

This page is meant to do one thing: give you a clear mental map of what’s going on, what usually matters in care decisions, and how to use the rest of Maisage without feeling like you’re already behind.

You don’t have to get everything “right” today. The goal here is to get your bearings, not to pick a perfect plan on the first try.

Who this is for

This page is especially for you if any of these feel familiar:

  • You’ve had a hospital stay, fall, or new diagnosis and everything sped up overnight.
  • Family members are pushing different directions and you’re stuck in the middle.
  • You’re being shown options (home care, assisted living, rehab, “respite”) that all blur together.
  • The money side and the medication side both feel like a black box.

If that’s where you are, you’re not behind. You’re in the exact place this page was written for.

The basics of most care decisions

Every situation is different, but most big care decisions boil down to a few repeating pieces. Seeing those pieces clearly is often more important than finding the “perfect” option.

1. People

Who is involved now, and who is realistically available?

  • The person needing care (what do they want, fear, understand?).
  • Family or close friends and what they can actually do week to week.
  • Doctors, nurses, social workers, pharmacists, and other professionals already on the team.

A clear picture of roles often matters more than any specific building or service.

2. Place

Where is care happening now, and what are the realistic options?

  • Staying in the current home with more support.
  • Moving to a different home or in with family.
  • Moving to assisted living, memory care, or another facility.

No place is perfect. Each location solves some problems and creates others.

3. Money

What can be sustained, not just paid for once?

  • Monthly income and savings that can reliably support care.
  • Insurance coverage limits and what happens after they’re reached.
  • Big one-time costs: moves, deposits, home changes, equipment.

The better question is usually “Which path can we sustain for 6–24 months?” not “What’s the cheapest or fanciest thing we can manage once?”

4. Medications

Are medications helping, confusing things, or quietly driving cost and risk?

  • How many medications are active and who is really managing them.
  • Which ones drive the pharmacy bill the most.
  • Whether the list reflects today’s needs or years of layering.

Medication plans should always be reviewed with prescribers and pharmacists — this site can help you prepare for that conversation.

5. Time

What timeline are you really planning for?

  • Short-term stabilization (weeks to a few months).
  • Medium-term “let’s see how this goes” (6–18 months).
  • Longer-term “if nothing major changes” (2+ years).

You don’t have to plan forever. Choosing a time frame helps keep conversations realistic and honest.

Common traps to watch for

You’re already under pressure. A few common patterns tend to make things worse. Knowing them ahead of time helps you push back calmly.

“You have to decide today.”

True emergencies exist, but most housing and service decisions are not one-day deadlines.

  • Ask what happens if you wait 24–72 hours.
  • Ask what specifically will change if you don’t sign immediately.

A decision you don’t understand is rarely a good decision, even if it’s fast.

“This is the only way.”

There is almost always more than one path — each with different costs and trade-offs.

  • Ask what other families in similar situations have done.
  • Ask what a “Plan B” or “Plan C” might look like.

You don’t need every option, just a small set of honest ones.

“Don’t worry about the details.”

If a contract, discharge plan, or marketing packet feels vague, it probably is.

  • Ask for specifics in writing: who, how often, what is included, what is extra.
  • Ask how to escalate concerns if promises aren’t met.

You’re allowed to slow things down long enough for the details to make sense.
