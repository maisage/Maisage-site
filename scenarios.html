Scenarios

Three example paths real families follow

These scenarios are not recommendations. They are simple, realistic patterns that show how care, money, and family involvement can interact over time. The goal is to help you see tradeoffs more clearly — not to choose for you.

All examples are simplified and use rounded ideas, not real client data. You should always confirm details for your own state, income, and health situation.

How to use these scenarios

  • Look for what sounds emotionally familiar — not just financially.
  • Notice who is doing what: you, family, paid helpers, facilities, programs.
  • Ask, “If we followed this path, what would be the biggest risk for us?”
  • Use this page together with the Plan and Learn pages.

These scenarios are for thinking and conversation. They are not financial, legal, or medical advice.

Scenario 1 – Medicaid spend-down

In this path, the person continues living largely as they are until money runs low, then spends down savings and assets to qualify for Medicaid-funded long-term care.

What it can look like

  • Stays at home or in current setting while private money lasts.
  • Pays privately for home care or facility until assets drop below limits.
  • Applies for Medicaid when savings are nearly exhausted.
  • May need to move to a facility that accepts Medicaid once approved.

Where this path can fit

  • There is limited or no family support available.
  • Assets and income are modest and will not last long-term.
  • The priority is maximum care coverage once funds are gone.

Tradeoffs and risks

  • Less control over which facility or provider is available under Medicaid.
  • Estate recovery may claim some assets after death, depending on state rules.
  • Families may feel rushed when the money finally runs out.

Questions to ask if this sounds familiar

  • How long will current savings likely last at today’s spending level?
  • Which facilities nearby accept Medicaid, and what are their wait lists like?
  • What does your state’s Medicaid estate recovery program look like?

Scenario 2 – Family support hybrid

In this path, family members share some of the support — time, money, or both — to stretch independence and delay a full spend-down to Medicaid or a facility move.

What it can look like

  • Person stays at home or in a small apartment.
  • Adult children or relatives help with errands, meals, or appointments.
  • Paid helpers fill in gaps for tasks family can’t reliably cover.
  • Family may contribute monthly funds to cover part of care costs.

Where this path can fit

  • There are a few family members willing to coordinate and communicate.
  • Income or savings alone are not enough for full private-pay care.
  • The person strongly prefers to remain at home as long as safely possible.

Tradeoffs and risks

  • Family burnout if roles and limits are not clearly discussed.
  • Uneven contributions can create resentment over time.
  • Unexpected health changes can make the family plan no longer safe or realistic.

Questions to ask if this sounds familiar

  • What can each family member realistically commit to — and for how long?
  • How will you track who is doing what, so no one feels taken for granted?
  • What’s the backup plan if a key helper becomes unavailable?
  • Are you comfortable being honest about limits before resentment builds?

Scenario 3 – Reverse equity blend

In this path, home equity or other assets are purposely used to fund care and independence, often through selling the home, downsizing, or using borrowing tools like a reverse mortgage (where appropriate and fully understood).

What it can look like

  • Person sells a large home and moves to a smaller, safer place.
  • Freed-up equity is used deliberately to pay for home help or assisted living.
  • In some cases, a reverse mortgage is used — after understanding fees and risks.
  • Family may coordinate to balance “inheritance later” vs “quality of life now.”

Where this path can fit

  • There is meaningful home equity or assets that could be converted to income.
  • The person is open to moving or restructuring how assets are used.
  • The family can have honest discussions about tradeoffs between legacy and current care.

Tradeoffs and risks

  • Less inheritance or reduced estate for heirs.
  • Complex products (like reverse mortgages) can be expensive or misused if not well understood.
  • Once certain assets are sold or borrowed against, reversing the decision can be difficult.

Questions to ask if this sounds familiar

  • Have you clearly compared costs and terms from multiple providers?
  • Is everyone clear on how much equity might be used — and for what?
  • What does “enough for you to feel secure” look like before thinking about inheritance?
  • Have you checked independent, non-sales-based explanations of any product being considered?

Putting the three paths side by side

Scenario Main idea Key risk
Medicaid spend-down Use private funds until gone, then rely on Medicaid. Less control over options once on Medicaid; possible estate recovery.
Family support hybrid Blend family help, private pay, and lower-cost supports. Burnout or conflict if expectations aren’t clear and realistic.
Reverse equity blend Intentionally use assets or home equity to fund care and options. Reduced inheritance; risk if products are not fully understood.

These descriptions are simplified. Real-life plans often combine elements from more than one scenario.
