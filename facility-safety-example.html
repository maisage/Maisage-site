Facility Safety Signals – Example View

This example shows how Maisage could combine weekly and monthly public data into a simple view for one facility. The numbers here are sample values, not live data. The goal is to help families see patterns that matter, not just star ratings.

All information would come from public sources such as CMS, state health agencies, EMS / fire logs, police call logs, and court records. No resident-level information or private health data is used.

Staffing stability

High concern
  • RN hours: 0.49 hrs/resident/day (down from 0.78 three months ago).
  • Total nurse staffing: 3.1 hrs/resident/day (below state average).
  • Weekend coverage: consistently lower than weekdays.
  • Turnover: high nurse aide turnover in past 12 months.

Why it matters: Sudden staffing drops often show up weeks or months before formal inspection reports or star rating changes.

EMS / emergency activity

Elevated
  • EMS calls (last 30 days): 12 (↑ from 4 in the prior 30 days).
  • Common reasons: falls with injury, breathing problems, unresponsive resident.
  • Fire / alarm calls: 2 triggered evacuations, no fire damage reported.

Why it matters: Frequent 911 calls may mean residents are not being monitored or supported early enough to prevent emergencies.

Serious reportable incidents

Mixed
  • State incident reports (90 days): 4 filed with state health agency.
  • Types (example): 1 suspected neglect, 1 elopement, 2 significant falls.
  • Abuse / neglect findings: no substantiated abuse in past 12 months, 1 neglect finding 18 months ago.

Why it matters: State incident reports often appear before full inspection reports and can show patterns of risk.

Regulatory & legal actions

Stable
  • CMS enforcement: no current fines or payment bans reported in the past 12 months.
  • Ownership: no ownership changes in past 3 years.
  • Court filings: no new public negligence or wrongful death cases in the last 12 months (example).

Why it matters: Patterns of fines, ownership changes, and repeated lawsuits can point to deeper stability or culture issues.

Annual inspection and quality context

Most recent full health inspection (example): October 2023 · 8 total deficiencies · none at harm (G–L) level.

Key areas cited: care planning, documentation, and call light response times.

Quality measures (summary): hospital readmissions are slightly above state average; pressure ulcers and falls with major injury are within average range.

These annual and quarterly measures are helpful background, but they often reflect problems months after they start. Weekly and monthly signals above can give earlier warning.

How to use this kind of information

This type of view is meant to help you ask better questions, not to make decisions for you. Even when signals look concerning, you can still visit, observe, and speak directly with staff and leadership.

  • Ask about recent staffing changes and how they’re being addressed.
  • Ask how the facility reviews emergency calls and prevents repeat events.
  • Ask how incidents are reported to the state and what was done afterward.
  • Ask how leadership monitors patterns over time, not just individual events.

Maisage’s goal is to filter and translate public information so you can see patterns that matter and feel more prepared, not more frightened.
