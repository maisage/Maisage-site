Medicaid and long-term care

Medicaid is a joint federal–state program that can pay for long-term care when certain financial and eligibility rules are met. For many people, it becomes the default plan only after savings are gone — often without much advance planning.

Rules vary by state and change over time. This page gives general concepts only. For your specific situation, state Medicaid offices and qualified professionals are essential resources.

Basic ideas behind Medicaid and long-term care

In many states, Medicaid is a primary payer for long-term nursing facility care. To qualify, a person usually must:

  • Meet medical or functional criteria (needing a certain level of care).
  • Have income and assets below specific limits, with some protections for a spouse.
  • Follow rules about transfers and gifts within a “lookback” period.

Details such as asset limits, protections for a spouse, and home treatment differ by state.

What “spend-down” means

Many families hear the phrase “you have to spend down to qualify for Medicaid.” In practice, this can mean:

  • Paying for care and other allowed expenses until assets fall below Medicaid thresholds.
  • Converting certain resources into exempt forms (according to state rules).
  • Tracking timing to avoid penalties related to gifts or transfers made within the lookback period.

Because the rules are technical, many people choose to consult with elder law or Medicaid planning professionals when they approach this stage.

Estate recovery and why it matters

In many cases, states are required to seek recovery of certain Medicaid payments from a person’s estate after death, especially for long-term care services.

  • Estate recovery rules vary by state and can depend on factors like surviving spouses or disabled children.
  • In some situations, claims may be delayed or waived; in others, they may result in liens or claims on property.

Understanding estate recovery early can help families avoid surprises later.

Using Medicaid as one part of the plan

Medicaid can be an essential safety net, but relying on “Medicaid will take over” as the only plan can leave you with fewer choices and more stress. Many families:

  • Use private pay and family support earlier in the journey.
  • Plan for a possible Medicaid phase later, with realistic expectations.
  • Talk openly about what matters most if choice becomes more limited.

Understanding the basics now makes it easier to fit Medicaid into a broader plan instead of turning to it only in an emergency.
