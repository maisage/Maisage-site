6–24 Month Care Scenarios

Compare “stay home vs. move” without guessing

This is a thinking tool. You’re not promising anything to anyone. You’re just making the trade-offs visible.

Step 1 – Name your likely paths

Most families are really choosing between 2–3 realistic paths, for example:

  • Path A – Stay at home with increased family help and some paid support.
  • Path B – Move to assisted living within 6–12 months.
  • Path C – Short-term home focus, then reassess after rehab or stabilization.

Write your own A/B/C in simple language you’d use with a friend.

Step 2 – List the work in each path

For each path, sketch out:

  • Daily tasks: meds, meals, bathing, transfers, bathroom trips, night checks.
  • Weekly tasks: appointments, groceries, laundry, cleaning, driving.
  • Emotional load: supervision, confusion, conflict, family strain.

Then note who would actually be doing these in each path: which family members, what paid care, and what you hope the facility would take over.

Step 3 – Add rough money to the picture

You don’t need perfect math to see patterns. For each path:

  • List rent/mortgage, utilities, food, and transportation that stay the same.
  • Estimate care costs (home care hours, facility monthly rate, co-pays).
  • Note one-time costs (moves, deposits, equipment, home modifications).

The question is not “Can we afford perfection?” but “Which trade-offs are we willing to live with?”

Step 4 – Turn it into a one-page summary

For each path, write 3–5 bullet points on:

  • What will likely feel better in this path.
  • What will likely feel harder or riskier.
  • What would have to be true for this path to work for at least 6–12 months.

This one-page comparison is what you show family, doctors, or advisors when you say, “Here’s how I’m seeing our options. What am I missing?”

Medication Cost & Complexity

Find affordable medication options

A straightforward way to understand which medications are driving costs, where substitutions might be possible, and what questions to ask your prescribers and pharmacist. No clinical decisions are made here — this is for clarity and preparation.

1. Identify your likely cost drivers

High pharmacy bills usually come from a small set of medications. Use this checklist to narrow where the cost pressure is coming from:

  • Brand-name medications with no generic equivalent.
  • Combination medications that can be split into cheaper single agents.
  • Inhalers, injectables, patches, or specialty drugs.
  • Drugs on higher insurance tiers (Tier 3–5).
  • Older prescriptions that haven’t been reviewed in a while.

You don’t need exact prices yet. Start by marking which of your meds fall into these categories.

2. Questions to bring to your prescriber

These questions help you safely clarify options without suggesting changes yourself:

  • “Is there a generic or therapeutically equivalent alternative to this?”
  • “Is this medication still necessary, or is it left over from an older plan?”
  • “If cost is a concern, which of these could we review first?”
  • “Are any of these interacting with each other in a way that increases risk or cost?”

You are not asking for different medications — you’re asking which conversations matter most.

3. Compare prices the simple way

Prices vary widely across pharmacies. A practical workflow:

  • List your medications with dose and quantity.
  • Check prices at: Costco, Walmart, CVS/Walgreens, and at least one supermarket pharmacy.
  • Check a discount card price (GoodRx, WellRx, etc.) and compare to your insurance copay.
  • Flag any medication where paying cash is cheaper than using insurance.

You do not need to switch pharmacies yet — this step simply shows where the biggest gaps might be.
