Living Map

Build a “Living Map” of your real options — not just averages

National averages don’t decide your life. Your decisions happen in a specific city, with specific providers, at today’s prices. This page helps you build your own “living map” of care options, costs, and supports where you actually live.

Maisage doesn’t host a master database of prices. Instead, we show you how to collect and organize what you find from public sources, facility websites, and conversations — in a way you can update over time.

How to build your Living Map

  1. Pick the area that actually matters (for example: county, metro area, or radius from family).
  2. List the kinds of supports you might realistically use — at home and outside the home.
  3. Use public sources, provider sites, and phone calls to fill in rough ranges and notes.
  4. Update over time instead of starting over every time something changes.

You don’t need perfect data. You need a clear enough picture to compare options and ask better questions.

Step 1 – Define your real “map area”

Most families are not choosing between all 50 states. They’re choosing between a few cities, neighborhoods, or regions that keep them close enough to support and medical care.

Questions to answer:

  • Which cities or zip codes are truly realistic for you?
  • Who needs to be within driving distance — children, siblings, close friends?
  • Are there hospitals or medical networks you’d strongly prefer to stay near?

Step 2 – List the types of support you might use

Your Living Map is not just about facilities. It includes anything that helps you stay safe, connected, and supported — at home or elsewhere.

Home-based supports

  • Non-medical home care / aides
  • Home health (short-term skilled care)
  • Meal delivery or community meals
  • Transportation or ride programs
  • Adult day programs

Housing & facility options

  • Senior apartments / independent living
  • Assisted living
  • Memory care
  • Nursing facilities
  • Hospice providers (home-based or facility partners)

Step 3 – Capture what you find on one page

The goal is not to record every detail, but to capture enough to compare options side by side. You can start with a simple table like this and fill it in as you go.

The entries above are examples of how to structure your notes, not recommendations or real prices. You should always verify directly with providers.

Where to look for local information

Building your map usually means combining information from multiple places. Start with sources that are as neutral and public as possible, then layer in specific providers.

  • State or county aging agencies and long-term care ombudsman programs.
  • Non-profit organizations that publish local or regional cost-of-care summaries.
  • Facility and agency websites (“pricing” or “what’s included” pages).
  • Direct phone calls asking for “typical ranges” and “what’s not included.”

Maisage does not rate or endorse specific providers. The focus is on helping you collect and compare what you find in a structured way.

Keeping your Living Map alive

Prices change. Programs open and close. People move. The value of a Living Map is that it can be updated without starting from zero every time.

Simple habits that help:

  • Write down the “as of” month and year when you record a price or range.
  • Note who you spoke with at a facility, if you called.
  • Update one section at a time — for example, just home care providers this month.
  • Review the map after any major health or housing change.
