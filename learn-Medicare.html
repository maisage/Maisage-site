Medicare basics and Medicare Advantage

Medicare is the federal health insurance program for most people age 65 and older and some younger people with disabilities. It covers many medical services, but it does not cover everything, and it does not automatically cover long-term custodial care.

This page gives a plain-language overview. It is not legal or financial advice. For detailed, current rules and options, always check official Medicare resources or speak with a qualified professional.

Original Medicare at a glance

Original Medicare is made up of:

  • Part A – hospital insurance (inpatient hospital, some skilled nursing, some home health, hospice).
  • Part B – medical insurance (doctor visits, outpatient care, certain tests, durable medical equipment).

Many people add a Part D drug plan and may purchase a Medigap (supplement) policy to help with deductibles and coinsurance. These add-ons have separate rules and costs.

How Medicare Advantage is different

Medicare Advantage (Part C) plans are offered by private companies that contract with Medicare. When you enroll in a Medicare Advantage plan, the plan becomes your main way of receiving Medicare-covered services.

  • Plans often include drug coverage and extra benefits like vision or dental.
  • They may use networks and require referrals or prior authorizations.
  • Your out-of-pocket costs and rules depend on the specific plan you choose.

Advantage plans can be helpful for some people and limiting for others. The details of the plan matter more than the advertising headline.

What Medicare generally does not cover

Medicare is mainly designed for medical and short-term skilled care. It generally does not cover:

  • Long-term custodial care in a nursing facility (help with daily activities on an ongoing basis).
  • Most assisted living or memory care room and board.
  • 24-hour in-home custodial care.

This gap is one reason many people end up relying on a mix of private pay, family help, long-term care insurance (if available), or Medicaid later in life.

Questions to ask when reviewing options

  • Are my doctors and preferred hospitals in-network (if considering an Advantage plan)?
  • What are the maximum out-of-pocket costs in a year under this plan?
  • How does the plan handle prior authorizations for common services?
  • Would I be comfortable with the tradeoff between extra benefits and network rules?

These questions can help you look beyond monthly premiums and focus on how the plan might work in real situations.
