Medicare & Medicare Advantage in plain language

Medicare is a federal health insurance program, mainly for people 65 and older and for some younger people with disabilities. This page offers a simplified overview of the major parts of Medicare and how Medicare Advantage differs from Original Medicare.

For detailed, official information, always refer to Medicare.gov, Social Security, or your plan’s own documents. Rules and options can change over time.

Two main paths: Original Medicare and Medicare Advantage

At a high level, there are two main ways to receive Medicare coverage: staying with Original Medicare or enrolling in a Medicare Advantage plan.

Original Medicare

  • Part A – hospital coverage.
  • Part B – medical and outpatient coverage.
  • You can add a separate drug plan (Part D).
  • You can buy a supplemental policy (Medigap) in many states to help with deductibles and coinsurance.
  • Generally allows you to see providers who accept Medicare across the country.

Medicare Advantage (Part C)

  • Offered by private insurance companies approved by Medicare.
  • Must cover at least what Original Medicare covers.
  • Often includes drug coverage and may offer extra benefits (such as dental or vision).
  • Typically uses provider networks (HMO, PPO, etc.).
  • May require prior authorizations or referrals for some services.

Official information and tools about Parts A, B, C, and D, plus Medigap policies and enrollment windows, are available at Medicare.gov.

Looking more closely at Medicare Advantage (Part C)

Medicare Advantage plans are an alternative way to receive Medicare benefits through private insurance companies. Instead of Original Medicare paying providers directly, the plan is paid by Medicare to manage your coverage.

What these plans typically do

  • Combine hospital, medical, and often drug coverage into a single plan.
  • Set their own copays, coinsurance, and out-of-pocket limits within Medicare rules.
  • Use provider networks (you may pay more or have no coverage out of network, depending on the plan).
  • May require prior authorization for certain tests, procedures, or medications.

Things to pay attention to

  • Whether your doctors, hospitals, and pharmacies are in-network.
  • How your medications are covered (tiers, prior authorizations, quantity limits).
  • The annual out-of-pocket maximum and how it is calculated.
  • Rules for getting care while traveling or outside your usual service area.

Plan details can change each year. For current, plan-specific information, use the Medicare Plan Finder, your plan’s official materials, or your State Health Insurance Assistance Program (SHIP).

Use this as a starting point

This page is meant to help you understand overall structure — not to recommend one path over another. What works best depends on your health needs, finances, where you live, and which providers you want to keep.

Always confirm details with official sources such as Medicare.gov, your plan’s documents, your state Medicaid office, or licensed professionals before making decisions.
