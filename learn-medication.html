Medications: cost and complexity

This page is here so the medication list and pharmacy counter feel less like a black box. It explains why some medications drive cost and confusion, and what levers families sometimes have.

Why some medications feel “out of control”

For most people, the pattern looks like this:

  • Many medications are cheap generics.
  • A few are surprisingly expensive or jump in price without warning.
  • Different pharmacies quote different prices for the same prescription.

The price you see at the counter is shaped by list prices, insurance rules, pharmacy benefit managers (PBMs), and whether your specific plan prefers that drug or not.

Tiers and formularies, in plain language

Most insurance plans use a formulary — a list of drugs they prefer — and divide them into cost tiers:

  • Lower tiers: usually generics and lower-cost preferred medications.
  • Higher tiers: brand-name or more expensive drugs.
  • Non-formulary: not favored by the plan; often highest cost or requires special approval.

When one drug is very expensive, it’s often because of where it sits on that ladder, not because a particular pharmacy is targeting you.

What this page can and can’t do

You cannot use this page to safely change medications on your own. You can use it to:

  • Understand why some drugs cost so much more than others.
  • Identify which medications to focus on when talking to prescribers.
  • Prepare questions to bring to appointments and the pharmacy.

For concrete tools to map your list, compare prices, and write down questions, see the Medications section on the Tools page.

Good questions to ask about any expensive medication

  • “Are there lower-cost alternatives that would still be appropriate for this condition?”
  • “Is this still needed at this dose and frequency?”
  • “If this is on a higher tier, are there preferred options my plan usually covers?”
  • “If we can’t change this one, are there other medications that could be simplified?”
