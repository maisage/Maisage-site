Community – you’re not the only one figuring this out

Using Maisage with your family and care team

This page is about how to bring other people into the process — without losing control, getting steamrolled, or turning every conversation into a fight about who’s “doing enough.”

Share the work, not just the stress

Caring for someone almost always involves more than one person — siblings, spouses, adult children, friends, neighbors, paid caregivers, and professionals. Maisage can help you put shared reality on the table.

Ways to use this site together
  • Fill out the Start Here snapshot, then email or text a PDF to key family members and ask, “Does this match how you see things?”
  • Use the Tools page during a family call to compare “stay home vs. move” scenarios in real time.
  • Bring Facility Insights notes to tours and ask staff about specific patterns you noticed.
  • Use the medication view as a shared list between family and whoever refills or organizes meds.
You don’t need everyone to agree on everything. It’s enough to agree on what’s happening and what the realistic options are.

Hard conversations with less chaos

The data and tools don’t replace hard conversations, but they give you something concrete to point to when emotions are high.

Examples of things you might say
“I’m not saying we have to choose this option. I just want us to look at what the next 6–12 months might actually feel like if we keep things exactly as they are.”
“Can we look at these three facilities side-by-side based on inspections and staffing first — before we get swayed by the décor and sales pitch?”
“I’m at my limit on nights and weekends. Here’s what I can realistically keep doing, and here’s what I can’t.”
Tips that tend to help
  • Talk about the situation, not who is “good” or “bad” at caregiving.
  • Use “I” statements: “I’m worried about…”, “I notice…”, “I can commit to…”
  • Stay anchored to what the person needing care would want if they could see the full picture.

When family members disagree

Disagreement doesn’t automatically mean you’re doing something wrong. It often means people are scared, tired, or holding different pieces of the story.

  • Use written snapshots (from Start Here or the tools) to give everyone the same baseline information.
  • Ask, “What part of this do you see differently?” rather than “Why won’t you help?”
  • When needed, bring in a neutral third party: a care manager, social worker, or counselor.
If a dynamic feels abusive, manipulative, or unsafe, protecting yourself matters too. It’s okay to set limits, even in families that “don’t do that.”

What “community” means here

Many caregiver communities online are overloaded with ads, upsells, or unverified advice. Maisage is intentionally quieter.

The emphasis is on:

Clarity over hype No commissions Realistic options Respect for limits

As the project grows, any “community” features (forums, small groups, events) will be optional, privacy-minded, and clearly separated from advertising.

Future possibilities for this page

Over time, this space may include:

  • Short, anonymous stories about real decisions families have had to make.
  • Sample questions to bring to doctors, pharmacists, attorneys, and financial planners.
  • Office-hours–style small group sessions focused on specific topics (e.g., “first facility move,” “Medicaid and housing,” “sibling conflict”).

None of that exists yet. For now, this page is about helping you use the rest of the site with other people in your life.

Support that is not Maisage

There are times when you need more than planning tools:

  • Crisis or emergency: call local emergency services or your on-call medical team.
  • Mental health strain: consider talking with a therapist, counselor, or clergy you trust.
  • Legal conflict over care or money: consult an attorney who practices elder law in your state.

Community doesn’t mean you have to do everything within the family. Bringing in outside help is often the most loving move you can make.

IMPORTANT
Any future community features on Maisage will be moderated and optional. Nothing shared here should be treated as medical, legal, financial, or insurance advice, and it should never replace direct guidance from licensed professionals who know your situation.
